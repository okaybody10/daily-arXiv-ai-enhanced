### [1] [How Personas Can Influence Agents to Play Split or Steal](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05398)
*Carlos Leon,Alexandre Rodrigues,Pedro Gamito,Thomas D. Parsons*

Main category: cs.CL

TL;DR: Study tests how persona prompts and different open LLMs affect strategic behavior in an iterated Split-or-Steal social dilemma with a GPT-based virtual human opponent.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Persona prompting is widely used to steer LLM agents, but it is unclear how reliably personas shape strategic, cooperative vs. exploitative behavior in repeated social dilemmas, especially across different model families. The authors want to understand whether and how personas plus model choice change behavior in trust-like games to inform future human–AI interaction studies.

Method: They run 160 Portuguese-language sessions of a 15-round iterated Split or Steal game between persona-driven open-source LLM agents and a virtual human opponent driven by GPT-4.1 mini. Agents come from four open models (Ministral 3:3b, phi4:14b, Gemma3:12b, Gemma4:e4b) under three decoding regimes (temperature 0, 0.3, 0.7). Persona prompts are designed from Big Five personality traits (e.g., Prosocial, Principled, Analytical). They analyze outcome frequencies (split/split, split/steal, etc.), model effects, persona effects, topic content of dialogues, and sentiment labels.

Result: Overall, mutual cooperation (Split–Split) is very common (~74% of rounds) and exploitation events are rare (<11%). Model family matters: phi4 and Ministral 3:3b are stably cooperative across temperatures, while Gemma3:12b and Gemma4:e4b show more diverse and less consistently cooperative strategies. Prosocial and Principled personas are the most reliably cooperative; Analytical personas are more prone to exploiting the virtual human. Topic modeling shows friendship-related language associates with Split decisions, while money- and vengeance-related language appears more with Steal. Sentiment is mostly neutral or happy and does not add much explanatory signal beyond other features.

Conclusion: Persona prompts can influence strategic behavior in LLM agents, but their impact is intertwined with model-specific tendencies. Some persona–model combinations yield stable cooperation, while others produce more opportunistic behavior. Linguistic themes, more than coarse sentiment, align with cooperative vs. exploitative choices. The study provides a behavioral baseline for future VR experiments where humans interact with an embodied GPT-based virtual human in repeated trust games.

Abstract: Personas are often employed to guide large language model agents, yet their effectiveness in shaping strategic behavior in social dilemma settings remains uncertain. To address this, we examined the impact of persona prompts in an iterated Split or Steal game where persona-driven agents interacted with a Virtual Human (VH) controlled by a fixed prompt. Agents were instantiated from four open models (Ministral 3:3b, phi4:14b, Gemma3:12b, and Gemma4:e4b) at two temperature settings (0.3 and 0.7) and deterministic decision with zero temperature, while the VH was powered by GPT 4.1 mini. Across 160 sessions of 15 rounds each conducted in European Portuguese, mutual Split outcomes dominated (roughly 74 percent of rounds), with exploitation occurring in fewer than 11 percent of rounds. Model choice significantly influenced behavior: phi4 and Ministral 3:3b remained consistently cooperative across temperatures, whereas Gemma3:12b and Gemma4:e4b exhibited more varied strategies and outcomes. Analyses based on Big Five personality traits indicated that Prosocial and Principled personas were most consistently cooperative, while Analytical personas were more likely to exploit the VH. Topic analysis revealed that friendship-related dialogue aligns with Split decisions, whereas money and vengeance-related content is more prevalent in Steal outcomes; sentiment labels were predominantly neutral or happy and provided limited additional explanatory value. These findings characterize the interaction between persona prompts and model differences in repeated trust games and serve as a baseline for planned virtual reality studies involving human participants interacting with an embodied VH.

### [2] [Benchmarking KV-Cache Optimizations across Task Quality and System Performance for Long-Context Serving](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05399)
*Nikita Agrawal,Ruben Mayer*

Main category: cs.CL

TL;DR: They build a unified benchmark to fairly compare different KV-cache compression methods for long-context LLM serving and show that compression ratio alone doesn’t predict real performance; the best method depends on the workload.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: KV-cache memory grows linearly with context length and is becoming the main bottleneck for serving long-context LLMs. Many compression methods exist (quantization, pruning, merging), but they’ve been evaluated in inconsistent setups—different models, tasks, and metrics—so practitioners lack clear guidance on which technique to use for which workload.

Method: They design a workload-aware benchmark built on LongBench-style tasks (multi/single-document QA, few-shot learning, summarization) and implement several representative KV-cache optimization methods (KIVI, TurboQuant, SnapKV, CaM) in a shared serving stack. They evaluate these on Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3, measuring not only task quality but also mean output throughput, mean time-to-first-token, and realized compression ratio across various context-length buckets.

Result: They find notable discrepancies between nominal compression ratios and actual end-to-end performance. KIVI delivers the most stable task quality across both models. SnapKV achieves the highest throughput for long-context scenarios. CaM can give very large gains on some QA workloads but its performance and realized compression ratio vary significantly by workload. Overall, no single method dominates across all settings.

Conclusion: Effective KV-cache compression for long-context LLM serving must be selected with awareness of the target workload and performance objectives; simple metrics like compression ratio are insufficient. Their benchmark offers comparative evidence and practical guidance, arguing against one-size-fits-all KV-cache strategies and in favor of tailored, workload-specific choices in deployment.

Abstract: Large language model serving is increasingly limited by KV-cache growth under long-context workloads, yet existing KV-cache compression techniques are difficult to compare because they were evaluated on different models, tasks, budgets, and serving stacks. This paper presents a workload-aware benchmark of representative KV-cache optimization mechanisms spanning quantization, pruning, and merging, including KIVI, TurboQuant, SnapKV, and CaM, evaluated on LongBench-style multi-document QA, single-document QA, few-shot learning, and summarization workloads using Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3. The benchmark measures task quality, mean output throughput, mean time-to-first-token, and realized compression ratio across context-length buckets. The results show that the compression ratio alone is a poor predictor of end-to-end performance. KIVI4 provides the most stable quality across models, SnapKV delivers the strongest long-context throughput, and CaM yields large gains on selected QA workloads but exhibits substantial workload sensitivity in both quality and realized compression ratio. ### [4] [Most LLM Conformity Needs No Speaker: Measuring the Speaker-Free Floor in Peer-Pressure Benchmarks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05545)
*Yibo Hu,Jiaming Qu*

Main category: cs.CL

TL;DR: The paper argues that much of what appears as LLM “conformity” to peer answers is actually driven by repeated exposure to the wrong answer itself, not by social-source cues, and proposes a new evaluation setup that separates these effects.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Prior work claims that LLMs exhibit social conformity: changing correct answers to match peers or groups. However, existing benchmarks confound two factors: the presence of a source (speaker, group, expert) and the repetition of a particular answer. Because these are varied together, it is unclear whether revisions are due to social influence or simply the effect of seeing the same answer repeated. The authors aim to disentangle these effects and provide a cleaner methodology for measuring genuine source-based conformity.

Method: They introduce a “no-source” condition in which the same asserted answer appears, but any explicit speaker or source framing is removed. Using six open-weight LLMs and seven QA/reasoning datasets, they compare: (1) a plain re-ask baseline, (2) the no-source repeated-answer condition, and (3) conditions with various source framings (e.g., expert panel vs minimal person labels). They also test robustness when the wrong answer is paraphrased and when answer options are hidden (open-ended setting). They analyze how often initially correct answers are revised, and with what confidence, across these conditions.

Result: In the no-source condition, LLMs revise correct answers to the wrong repeated answer in 66.5% of cases, much higher than the 10.3% revision rate under a plain re-ask. This harmful revision persists even when the repeated answer is paraphrased and when explicit options are hidden. Source framing mainly modulates this already-high “floor”: expert-panel framing increases harmful revision above the no-source level, whereas minimal person labels do not reliably increase it. When models flip, they tend to become confidently wrong, and simple confidence-based recalibration does not restore the original correct answer.

Conclusion: Most behavior previously interpreted as social conformity in LLMs is better explained by sensitivity to repeated textual assertions rather than to the presence or authority of a source. Source attribution still has an effect, but its impact should be measured as an incremental increase over the no-source baseline. Methodologically, conformity benchmarks should first quantify how much answer revision occurs when the source is removed; otherwise, they risk misattributing repetition effects to social influence and overestimating genuine conformity.

Abstract: LLM conformity is often used to describe cases where a model changes a correct answer toward a peer or group response. We show that most of this apparent conformity survives even after the peer is removed. The reason is a confound: standard conformity prompts mix two cues at once, the presence of a speaker and the repeated wrong answer itself. Existing benchmarks vary these cues together, so they cannot tell how much of the revision actually depends on the speaker. We introduce a no-source condition: the same asserted answer with the explicit speaker removed. Across six open-weight LLMs and seven QA and reasoning datasets, this condition alone causes harmful revision in $66.5\%$ of initially correct cases, compared with $10.3\%$ under a plain re-ask. The effect also remains when the repeated answer is paraphrased and when answer options are hidden in an open-ended setting. Source framing mainly modulates this floor: expert-panel framing raises it, while minimal person labels do not reliably raise it. When models flip, they are usually confidently wrong, and simple recalibration does not recover the original answer. Source attribution still matters, but it should be measured as an increment above this speaker-free floor. The methodological lesson is that conformity benchmarks should first measure what remains after the speaker is removed; without this step, benchmarks may mistake repeated text for social influence.

<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Existing work finds that LLMs change their binary moral judgments when prompts are reframed in logically irrelevant ways (e.g., wording, order, polarity). But in yes/no formats, the logical verdict, the lexical word ("yes"/"no"), and the on-screen position are confounded, making it hard to know whether models really change their underlying moral stance or just react to surface artifacts such as option order or specific tokens. A systematic method is needed to separate genuine moral preferences from framing-induced noise.

Method: The authors design a psychometric battery using "crossed symmetrization": for each moral dilemma, they generate multiple logically equivalent question forms where all irrelevant features (e.g., wording, option order, polarity) are flipped in balanced pairs. They collect graded ratings (not just yes/no) across these forms to infer an internal moral scale or stance parameter θ for each model. They also force models into binary yes/no verdicts in different formats to measure artifacts: order bias (preference for first vs. last option), lexical bias (attraction/repulsion to words like "no"), and label bias (using arbitrary labels instead of yes/no). They fit a minimal logistic model P = σ((θ ± m)/s) to summarize framing susceptibility m and moral decisiveness s, and compare behavior across multiple LLM families and sizes, including frontier and small open-weight models.

Result: Graded ratings across symmetrized formats reveal that frontier LLMs have a relatively stable internal moral stance θ that is almost format-invariant, with low cross-form incoherence (0.12–0.21 on a ±1 scale). Smaller open-weight models show larger, model-specific inconsistencies. When constrained to yes/no verdicts, the observed framing effects decompose into two main artifacts: (1) an order bias toward the last-printed option (a recency effect contrary to human primacy) and (2) a lexical bias toward the token "no." These artifacts are strong in Claude models (story-averaged biases from -0.32 to -0.86), near zero for GPT-5.5 and Gemini, and diminish when prompts elicit extended reasoning. Replacing "yes"/"no" with arbitrary labels decouples lexical and logical aspects: the logical verdict bias is ~0 for all frontier models, but model-specific label and order biases persist, driven by surface form rather than semantic rejection or acceptance. The logistic model parameters m and s effectively capture these effects and are distinguishable from standard sampling temperature.

Conclusion: The paper concludes that LLMs, especially frontier models, possess a coherent underlying moral preference structure that is largely stable across logically equivalent framings when measured with graded ratings. Apparent shifts in binary moral judgments mostly reflect decomposable surface artifacts tied to option order and lexical tokens, not genuine changes in moral stance. Therefore, reliable measurement of what a model “values” requires systematically crossing and balancing question frames instead of relying on a single yes/no prompt. The proposed psychometric battery and minimal model offer a general, format-agnostic way to quantify framing susceptibility and moral decisiveness across models and dilemma sets.

Abstract: Large language models (LLMs) increasingly issue judgments read as binary verdicts, and a growing literature reports such judgments shifting under logically irrelevant changes of wording - among them an amplified yes-no bias on moral dilemmas, absent in humans. A single framing cannot say what such a shift is: in a yes/no question the word "no" is at once logical verdict, lexical token, and last-printed option. We introduce a psychometric battery that separates these: crossed symmetrization - every logically irrelevant factor flipped in balanced pairs - across a corpus of question forms. A graded rating across logically equivalent forms recovers a coherent internal moral scale: frontier models' stance $θ$ is nearly format-invariant (cross-form incoherence 0.12-0.21 on a $\pm 1$ axis); small open-weight models fail in model-specific ways. The word and the verdict share one token; swapping the words for arbitrary labels separates them, and the verdict-attached logical bias proves $\approx 0$ for every frontier model, while model-specific label and order attachments remain: the models are not drawn toward rejecting - the pull follows the printed surface, not the verdict it carries. A minimal model, $P = σ((θ\pm m)/s)$, summarizes any such artifact by a framing susceptibility m and a moral decisiveness s, measurably distinct from sampling temperature.
