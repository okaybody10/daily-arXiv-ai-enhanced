### [1] [When Should a Language Model Trust Itself? Same-Model Self-Verification as a Conditional Confidence Signal](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.02915)
*Aditya Ajay Phalod*

Main category: cs.CL

TL;DR: The paper studies same-model self-verification as a confidence signal, comparing it with strong likelihood-based baselines for selective prediction, and finds its effectiveness is highly task- and model-dependent rather than generally superior.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: In selective prediction with language models, we want reliable confidence signals to decide when a model should abstain from answering. Same-model self-verification (having a model critique its own answer) is an appealing idea, but its value is unclear once we compare it against simple but strong likelihood-based confidence measures like average or summed log-likelihood, which may already work well. The paper aims to rigorously test whether self-verification truly adds value over these baselines.

Method: The authors implement same-model self-verification by prompting models to audit their own earlier answers. They compare this signal against two likelihood-based baselines, LL-AVG and LL-SUM, on two benchmarks: ARC-Challenge and TruthfulQA-MC. They run experiments across multiple model families and scales, and across different prompt variants. They evaluate both how well each method ranks answers by correctness and how well it supports abstention decisions, using AURC (area under the risk-coverage curve) and operating-point analyses.

Result: Performance of self-verification relative to likelihood-based baselines is strongly dependent on task and model. On ARC-Challenge, self-verification provides substantial gains over LL-AVG for some models, especially Phi-2 and Qwen models, with the largest improvement for Qwen-7B. On TruthfulQA-MC, the self-verification signal is less robust: smaller models exhibit prompt sensitivity, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-8B gets worse compared to LL-AVG, and LL-SUM frequently remains the best practical baseline. Overall, there is no consistent dominance of self-verification across settings.

Conclusion: Same-model self-verification should not be treated as a universal uncertainty estimator for language models. Instead, it functions as a conditional confidence signal whose usefulness is contingent on the specific task, model family, prompt design, and—importantly—the strength of the likelihood-based baseline it is compared against. Practitioners should benchmark it against strong baselines rather than assuming it provides general improvements in selective prediction.

Abstract: Same-model self-verification, prompting a model to audit its own predicted answer, is a plausible confidence signal for selective prediction, but its practical value remains unclear once strong likelihood-based baselines are taken seriously. We evaluate self-verification against two such baselines, LL-AVG and LL-SUM, on ARC-Challenge and TruthfulQA-MC across multiple model families, scales, and prompt variants. We measure not only correctness ranking, but also abstention quality through AURC and operating-point analyses. The results are sharply task- and model-dependent. On ARC-Challenge, self-verification substantially improves over LL-AVG for Phi-2 and the Qwen models, with the largest gains appearing in Qwen-7B. On TruthfulQA-MC, however, the signal is less reliable: smaller models can become prompt-sensitive, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-8B degrades relative to LL-AVG, and LL-SUM often remains the stronger practical baseline. We therefore do not treat self-verification as a general-purpose uncertainty estimator. In this setting, it is better understood as a conditional confidence signal whose value depends on task type, model family, prompt formulation, and, crucially, the baseline it must beat.

Main category: cs.CL

<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>

Ablating these attention modules significantly improves performance on negation-related questions. Mechanistically, they find strong evidence that both suppression and constructive mechanisms for negation are present, with the constructive mechanism playing the larger role in practice.

Conclusion: LLMs’ failures on negation-heavy tasks are not due to an absence of negation understanding but to later-layer shortcut behaviors. Abstract: We study how Large Language Models (LLMs) process negation mechanistically. First, we establish that even though open-weight models often provide wrong answers to questions involving negation, they do possess internal components that process negation correctly. Their poor accuracy is due to late-layer attention behavior that promotes simple shortcuts; ablating those attention modules greatly improves accuracy on negation-related questions. Second, we uncover how models process negation. We consider two hypotheses: models could use attention heads that attend to the phrase being negated and suppress related concepts, or they could directly construct a representation of the entire negative phrase (e.g., representing "not gas" as a vector that promotes liquids and solids). We apply a range of observational and causal interpretability techniques on Mistral-7B and Llama-3.1-8B to show that models implement both mechanisms, with the "constructive" mechanism being more prominent. Combined, our work deepens the understanding of LLMs' internals, highlighting construction-dominant computations and the coexistence of competing mechanisms within LLMs.
