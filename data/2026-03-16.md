### [1] [Task-Specific Knowledge Distillation via Intermediate Probes](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.12270)
*Ryan Brown,Chris Russell*

Main category: cs.CL

TL;DR: The paper proposes a new knowledge distillation framework for reasoning tasks that supervises students using predictions from small probes on the teacher’s hidden states instead of the teacher’s output logits, improving performance especially in low-data settings.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Standard knowledge distillation assumes the teacher’s final output distribution is a clean, high-quality signal, but for reasoning tasks this is often false: formatting, tokenization, and vocabulary projection can corrupt or hide the correct answer, even when it exists in intermediate representations. The authors aim to exploit the richer, more faithful internal states of LLMs to obtain better supervision without extra data or model modifications.

Method: The authors freeze the large teacher model and train lightweight probes on its hidden states at intermediate layers. These probes are trained to predict task labels directly from the hidden activations. During student training, instead of distilling from the teacher’s final output logits, the student is supervised using the probe’s predictions derived from the teacher representations. The approach is architecture-agnostic, requires no changes to teacher or student, and leverages cached representations to keep compute low.

Result: Across four reasoning benchmarks (AQuA-RAT, ARC Easy, ARC Challenge, and MMLU), the proposed framework consistently outperforms standard logit-based distillation, with especially strong gains when training data is limited. Probes on intermediate layers provide labels that are cleaner and less noisy than the teacher’s own outputs, leading to better student performance.

Conclusion: Using predictions from probes trained on a teacher’s internal representations is a more reliable supervision signal than using the teacher’s final output distribution for reasoning tasks. This distillation strategy denoises the training signal, improves student performance without architectural changes or additional data, and offers a practical way to better leverage large teacher models in resource-constrained settings.

Abstract: Knowledge distillation from large language models (LLMs) assumes that the teacher's output distribution is a high-quality training signal. On reasoning tasks, this assumption is frequently violated. A model's intermediate representations may encode the correct answer, yet this information is lost or distorted through the vocabulary projection, where prompt formatting and answer-token choices creates brittle, noisy outputs.
  We introduce \method{}, a distillation framework that bypasses this bottleneck by training lightweight probes on frozen teacher hidden states and using the probe's predictions, rather than output logits, as supervision for student training. This simple change yields consistent improvements across four reasoning benchmarks (AQuA-RAT, ARC Easy/Challenge, and MMLU), with gains most pronounced under limited data.
  Probes trained on intermediate representations provide cleaner labels than the teacher's own outputs, effectively denoising the distillation signal. \method{} requires no architectural changes to student or teacher, is architecture-agnostic, and adds minimal compute since probe training is cheap and teacher representations can be cached. By exploiting internal representations, \method{} enables practitioners to extract more value from large teacher models without additional training data or architectural complexity.

### [2] [Diagnosing Retrieval Bias Under Multiple In-Context Knowledge Updates in Large Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.12271)
*Boyu Qiao,Sean Guo,Xian Yang,Kun Li,Wei Zhou,Songlin Hu,Yunya Song*

Main category: cs.CL

TL;DR: The paper studies how large language models handle multiple sequential updates of the same fact in a long context and shows they are biased toward earlier information and struggle to reliably use the latest update.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Existing work mostly examines one-shot knowledge updates or single conflicts, but many real-world tasks require tracking repeated revisions of the same fact in one context; this multi-update setting is underexplored and resembles cognitive interference (AB-AC) effects in humans. The authors want to understand whether and how LLMs can track and follow successive knowledge updates.

Method: They propose the Dynamic Knowledge Instance (DKI) evaluation framework, where a factual cue is paired with a sequence of updated values over time. Models are tested via endpoint probing: querying about either the earliest (initial) or the latest (current) state after multiple updates. They run this on diverse LLMs and perform diagnostic analyses of attention patterns, hidden-state similarities, and output logits to see how internal signals behave when models succeed or fail. They also test simple, cognitively inspired heuristic interventions intended to reduce interference and improve use of the latest update.

Result: As the number of updates increases, retrieval bias grows: models increasingly favor older information. Accuracy on earliest-state queries remains high, but accuracy on latest-state queries drops markedly. The internal diagnostics show that attention distributions, hidden representations, and logits become flatter and less discriminative on error cases, offering little clear signal corresponding to the most recent update. Heuristic interventions provide only small improvements and do not remove the bias.

Conclusion: LLMs have a persistent difficulty in correctly tracking and prioritizing the latest version of a fact when multiple updates appear in a long context. Their internal signals do not provide a robust basis for selecting the most recent update, and simple, cognitively inspired fixes are insufficient, highlighting a fundamental challenge for knowledge-intensive, dynamically updated applications.

Abstract: LLMs are widely used in knowledge-intensive tasks where the same fact may be revised multiple times within context. Unlike prior work focusing on one-shot updates or single conflicts, multi-update scenarios contain multiple historically valid versions that compete at retrieval, yet remain underexplored. This challenge resembles the AB-AC interference paradigm in cognitive psychology: when the same cue A is successively associated with B and C, the old and new associations compete during retrieval, leading to bias. Inspired by this, we introduce a Dynamic Knowledge Instance (DKI) evaluation framework, modeling multi-updates of the same fact as a cue paired with a sequence of updated values, and assess models via endpoint probing of the earliest (initial) and latest (current) states. Across diverse LLMs, we observe that retrieval bias intensifies as updates increase, earliest-state accuracy stays high while latest-state accuracy drops substantially. This demonstrates that theory-guided, non-uniform sparsity allocation is a more robust path to efficient LLM inference than uniform or purely heuristic methods.
