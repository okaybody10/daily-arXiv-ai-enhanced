### [1] [Exons-Detect: Identifying and Amplifying Exonic Tokens via Hidden-State Discrepancy for Robust AI-Generated Text Detection](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.24981)
*Xiaowei Zhu,Yubing Ren,Fang Fang,Shi Wang,Yanan Cao,Li Guo*

Main category: cs.CL

TL;DR: Exons-Detect is a training-free method that improves AI-generated text detection by reweighting tokens based on hidden-state discrepancies between two models, achieving state-of-the-art performance and robustness.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Large language models make AI-generated text hard to distinguish from human writing, leading to risks like misinformation, unclear authorship, and IP concerns. Existing training-free detectors aggregate token-level signals but assume all tokens contribute equally, which harms robustness on short texts and under local edits. There is a need for a more fine-grained, token-sensitive detection method that remains training-free yet more reliable.

Method: Exons-Detect introduces an exon-aware token reweighting framework in a dual-model setting. It compares hidden-state representations from two models to measure token-level discrepancies, identifies informative 'exonic' tokens, and amplifies their influence via importance weighting. Using these reweighted tokens, it computes an interpretable translation score that serves as the detection statistic for AI-generated vs. human-written text, without additional training.

Result: Empirical experiments show Exons-Detect achieves state-of-the-art performance on AI-generated text detection benchmarks, including DetectRL, where it yields a 2.2% relative AUROC improvement over the strongest prior training-free baseline. It also maintains high performance across different input lengths and under adversarial text modifications, indicating strong robustness.

Conclusion: By focusing on exon-like informative tokens through hidden-state discrepancy and importance reweighting, Exons-Detect overcomes the uniform-token assumption of prior training-free detectors. It delivers better, more robust AI-text detection with interpretable scoring, suggesting exon-aware token weighting is a promising direction for future detection methods.

Abstract: The rapid advancement of large language models has increasingly blurred the boundary between human-written and AI-generated text, raising societal risks such as misinformation dissemination, authorship ambiguity, and threats to intellectual property rights. These concerns highlight the urgent need for effective and reliable detection methods. While existing training-free approaches often achieve strong performance by aggregating token-level signals into a global score, they typically assume uniform token contributions, making them less robust under short sequences or localized token modifications. To address these limitations, we propose Exons-Detect, a training-free method for AI-generated text detection based on an exon-aware token reweighting perspective. Exons-Detect identifies and amplifies informative exonic tokens by measuring hidden-state discrepancy under a dual-model setting, and computes an interpretable translation score from the resulting importance-weighted token sequence. Empirical evaluations demonstrate that Exons-Detect achieves state-of-the-art detection performance and exhibits strong robustness to adversarial attacks and varying input lengths. In particular, it attains a 2.2\% relative improvement in average AUROC over the strongest prior baseline on DetectRL.

### [2] [Resisting Humanization: Ethical Front-End Design Choices in AI for Sensitive Contexts](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.24853)
*Silvia Rossi,Diletta Huyskes,Mackenzie Jorgensen*

Main category: cs.AI

TL;DR: The paper argues that the ethical design of AI must include not only back-end issues (data, models, algorithms) but also front-end, human-facing interface choices, especially in conversational systems, because humanizing elements strongly shape users’ expectations, trust, and autonomy.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Current AI ethics discourse largely overlooks how front-end design and interaction patterns influence user perceptions, trust, and behavior. This is critical for conversational interfaces that use human-like traits, which can mislead users, particularly in sensitive contexts such as support for survivors of abuse.

Method: The authors conceptually analyze ‘humanization’ in AI interfaces using literature from HCI, conversational AI, and value-sensitive design, and ground their arguments in a case study of two NLP-based conversational systems developed by Chayn, a nonprofit that supports survivors and explicitly applies trauma-informed design principles.

Result: They show that humanized conversational interface elements (e.g., emotive language, anthropomorphic metaphors, personalities) can distort mental models, miscalibrate trust, and erode user autonomy, while the Chayn systems exemplify a contrasting approach of ethical restraint in interface design aligned with trauma-informed values.

Conclusion: Ethical AI must treat front-end interaction design as a central site of procedural ethics, not just an afterthought to back-end logic. By deliberately constraining or reshaping humanizing features in line with user values and vulnerabilities, designers can better protect users, especially in high-stakes, sensitive domains.

Abstract: Ethical debates in AI have primarily focused on back-end issues such as data governance, model training, and algorithmic decision-making. Less attention has been paid to the ethical significance of front-end design choices, such as the interaction and representation-based elements through which users interact with AI systems. This gap is particularly significant for Conversational User Interfaces (CUI) based on Natural Language Processing (NLP) systems, where humanizing design elements such as dialogue-based interaction, emotive language, personality modes, and anthropomorphic metaphors are increasingly prevalent. This work argues that humanization in AI front-end design is a value-driven choice that profoundly shapes users' mental models, trust calibration, and behavioral responses. Drawing on research in human-computer interaction (HCI), conversational AI, and value-sensitive design, we examine how interfaces can play a central role in misaligning user expectations, fostering misplaced trust, and subtly undermining user autonomy, especially in vulnerable contexts. To ground this analysis, we discuss two AI systems developed by Chayn, a nonprofit organization supporting survivors of gender-based violence. Chayn is extremely cautious when building AI that interacts with or impacts survivors by operationalizing their trauma-informed design principles. This Chayn case study illustrates how ethical considerations can motivate principled restraint in interface design, challenging engagement-based norms in contemporary AI products. We argue that ethical front-end AI design is a form of procedural ethics, enacted through interaction choices rather than embedded solely in system logic.

### [3] [Trace2Skill: Distill Trajectory-Local Lessons into Transferable Agent Skills](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.25158)
*Jingwei Ni,Yihao Liu,Xinpeng Liu,Yutao Sun,Mengyu Zhou,Pengyu Cheng,Dexin Wang,Xiaoxi Jiang,Guanjun Jiang*

Main category: cs.AI

TL;DR: Trace2Skill is a framework that automatically converts many agent trajectories into robust, reusable skills that transfer across models and tasks without retraining.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Manual design of domain-specific skills for LLM agents does not scale, while current automatic methods overfit to individual trajectories or shallow model knowledge, producing brittle skills. There is a need for a scalable way to transform rich execution experience into general, human-like declarative skills.

Method: Trace2Skill collects a diverse pool of trajectories from LLM agents and then uses a parallel set of sub-agents to analyze them. It first derives trajectory-specific lessons, then hierarchically consolidates these via inductive reasoning into a unified, conflict-free skill directory. The framework can refine existing human-written skills or construct new skills from scratch, and it is designed to be model-agnostic and parameter-free at deployment.

Result: In domains like spreadsheets, VisionQA, and math reasoning, Trace2Skill outperforms strong baselines, including Anthropic’s official xlsx skills. Skills learned by a 35B-parameter Qwen3.5 model significantly boosted the performance of a larger 122B-parameter Qwen3.5 model, achieving up to a 57.65-point absolute improvement on WikiTableQuestions and generalizing to out-of-distribution settings.

Conclusion: Complex agent experience can be distilled into compact, declarative skills that are robust, transferable across LLM sizes, and effective without parameter updates or external retrieval. Trace2Skill offers a scalable, human-analogous way to evolve and maintain skill libraries for LLM agents, enabling more capable and generalizable tool-using systems.

Abstract: Equipping Large Language Model (LLM) agents with domain-specific skills is critical for tackling complex tasks. Yet, manual authoring creates a severe scalability bottleneck. Conversely, automated skill generation often yields fragile or fragmented results because it either relies on shallow parametric knowledge or sequentially overfits to non-generalizable trajectory-local lessons. To overcome this, we introduce Trace2Skill, a framework that mirrors how human experts author skills: by holistically analyzing broad execution experience before distilling it into a single, comprehensive guide. Instead of reacting sequentially to individual trajectories, Trace2Skill dispatches a parallel fleet of sub-agents to analyze a diverse pool of executions. It extracts trajectory-specific lessons and hierarchically consolidates them into a unified, conflict-free skill directory via inductive reasoning. Trace2Skill supports both deepening existing human-written skills and creating new ones from scratch. Experiments in challenging domains, such as spreadsheet, VisionQA and math reasoning, show that Trace2Skill significantly improves upon strong baselines, including Anthropic's official xlsx skills. Crucially, this trajectory-grounded evolution does not merely memorize task instances or model-specific quirks: evolved skills transfer across LLM scales and generalize to OOD settings. For example, skills evolved by Qwen3.5-35B on its own trajectories improved a Qwen3.5-122B agent by up to 57.65 absolute percentage points on WikiTableQuestions. Ultimately, our results demonstrate that complex agent experience can be packaged into highly transferable, declarative skills -- requiring no parameter updates, no external retrieval modules, and utilizing open-source models as small as 35B parameters.
