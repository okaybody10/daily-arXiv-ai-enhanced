*Ghazal Kalhor,Yadollah Yaghoobzadeh*

Main category: cs.CL

TL;DR: The paper presents GhazalBench, a benchmark to test how large language models handle Persian ghazals, showing they understand meaning better than they recall exact verses, especially compared to English sonnets.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Persian poetry, especially canonical ghazals, is deeply embedded in Iranian cultural practices where people routinely quote, paraphrase, and complete verses from partial prompts. Existing LLM evaluations rarely test models’ ability to deal with this kind of culturally significant, form-sensitive text. The authors want a framework that checks not just semantic understanding but also precise surface-form recall and cue-based access, in order to better understand and improve LLM behavior on non-English poetic traditions.

Method: The authors construct GhazalBench, a benchmark centered on Persian ghazals. It comprises two main task families: (1) generating faithful prose paraphrases of poetic couplets to test semantic understanding, and (2) accessing canonical verses under different types of cues—some semantic, some formal, some partial—to test recall and completion. They evaluate multiple proprietary and open-weight multilingual LLMs on these tasks and compare performance patterns with a parallel benchmark built on English sonnets, thereby controlling for model architecture while varying language and training exposure.

Result: Across models, performance splits into two distinct dimensions: models are relatively strong at paraphrasing Persian ghazals, indicating good capture of poetic meaning, but they perform poorly at exact verse recall in completion-style tasks. When tasks are framed as recognition (e.g., choosing or identifying correct lines) rather than free completion, recall improves noticeably, narrowing the gap. In contrast, on the English sonnet benchmark, models show much stronger recall performance, implying that the Persian deficits are not due to inherent architectural issues. Instead, the differences point to training-data exposure as the main limiting factor.

Conclusion: GhazalBench reveals that current multilingual LLMs can often understand the meaning of Persian ghazals yet fail to reliably access and reproduce their exact canonical wording, especially under completion prompts. Recognition-style setups alleviate this problem but do not fully eliminate it, and comparisons with English sonnets underscore the role of uneven training exposure. The authors argue that robust evaluation of LLMs on culturally important texts must jointly examine semantic understanding, surface-form fidelity, and cue-dependent retrieval, and they offer GhazalBench as a public resource to support such work.

Abstract: Persian poetry plays an active role in Iranian cultural practice, where verses by canonical poets such as Hafez are frequently quoted, paraphrased, or completed from partial cues. Supporting such interactions requires language models to engage not only with poetic meaning but also with culturally entrenched surface form. We introduce GhazalBench, a benchmark for evaluating how large language models (LLMs) interact with Persian ghazals under usage-grounded conditions. GhazalBench assesses two complementary abilities: producing faithful prose paraphrases of couplets and accessing canonical verses under varying semantic and formal cues. Across several proprietary and open-weight multilingual LLMs, we observe a consistent dissociation: models generally capture poetic meaning but struggle with exact verse recall in completion-based settings, while recognition-based tasks substantially reduce this gap. A parallel evaluation on English sonnets shows markedly higher recall performance, suggesting that these limitations are tied to differences in training exposure rather than inherent architectural constraints. Our findings highlight the need for evaluation frameworks that jointly assess meaning, form, and cue-dependent access to culturally significant texts. GhazalBench is available at https://github.com/kalhorghazal/GhazalBench.
