  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Empirical scaling laws show that model performance improves predictably with more data, but it is unclear what intrinsic properties of real-world text corpora determine these laws. For each state, they compute a predictive contribution equal to its empirical probability mass times its KL divergence between the state’s next-token distribution and a global baseline next-token distribution. The residual tail mass beyond K(N) quantitatively tracks the remaining excess loss over the apparent irreducible baseline, across corpora and data scales.

Conclusion: The study supports a mechanistic view in which data scaling primarily advances an effective frontier through a data-intrinsic predictive state spectrum, rather than merely exhausting frequency tails of rare tokens. The global-KL contribution spectrum provides a compact corpus-level descriptor that both correlates with observed scaling exponents and, via its residual tail mass, accounts for remaining excess loss at finite data scales. This suggests that scaling laws can be understood in terms of progressive coverage of structured predictive states in the data, offering a principled way to link corpus statistics and model learning curves and potentially to predict scaling behavior for new datasets or architectures.
