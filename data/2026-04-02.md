### [2] [LinearARD: Linear-Memory Attention Distillation for RoPE Restoration](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.00004)
*Ning Yang,Hengyu Zhong,Wentao Wang,Baoliang Tian,Haijun Zhang,Jun Wang*

Main category: cs.CL

TL;DR: LinearARD is a self-distillation method that restores short-context performance in RoPE-scaled, long-context LLMs by enforcing attention-structure consistency using a memory-efficient linear kernel.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Extending LLM context windows via RoPE scaling and continual pre-training enables long-sequence processing but typically degrades performance on standard short-text tasks and requires large amounts of training tokens. There is a need for a method that preserves or restores original model capabilities while supporting long contexts, without heavy compute or data requirements.

Method: LinearARD uses a frozen teacher model with native RoPE and a student model with scaled RoPE for long contexts. Instead of matching hidden states, it aligns the internal attention behavior by supervising the row-wise distributions of dense Q/Q, K/K, and V/V self-relation matrices between teacher and student. To make this feasible for long sequences, it introduces a linear-memory kernel that avoids storing full n×n relation maps. This kernel relies on per-token log-sum-exp statistics and integrates logit recomputation into the backward pass, allowing exact computation of KL divergence and gradients with linear memory cost.

Result: Applied to LLaMA2-7B extended from a 4K to 32K context window, LinearARD recovers 98.3% of the short-text performance of strong baselines and exceeds them on long-context benchmarks. It attains these results with only 4.25M training tokens, far fewer than the 256M tokens used by LongReD and standard CPT approaches.

Conclusion: Attention-structure self-distillation via LinearARD can effectively restore and even improve the capabilities of long-context RoPE-scaled LLMs while being highly data-efficient. By supervising Q/Q, K/K, and V/V relation distributions through a linear-memory kernel, the method avoids the typical trade-off between long-context ability and short-context performance and substantially reduces training data requirements.

Abstract: The extension of context windows in Large Language Models is typically facilitated by scaling positional encodings followed by lightweight Continual Pre-Training (CPT). While effective for processing long sequences, this paradigm often disrupts original model capabilities, leading to performance degradation on standard short-text benchmarks. We propose LinearARD, a self-distillation method that restores Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE)-scaled students through attention-structure consistency with a frozen native-RoPE teacher. Rather than matching opaque hidden states, LinearARD aligns the row-wise distributions of dense $Q/Q$, $K/K$, and $V/V$ self-relation matrices to directly supervise attention dynamics. To overcome the quadratic memory bottleneck of $n \times n$ relation maps, we introduce a linear-memory kernel. This kernel leverages per-token log-sum-exp statistics and fuses logit recomputation into the backward pass to compute exact Kullback-Leibler divergence and gradients. On LLaMA2-7B extended from 4K to 32K, LinearARD recovers 98.3\% of the short-text performance of state-of-the-art baselines while surpassing them on long-context benchmarks. Notably, our method achieves these results using only \textbf{4.25M} training tokens compared to the \textbf{256M} tokens required by LongReD and CPT. Our code is available at https://github.com/gracefulning/LinearARD.

Method: The authors run four experiments using 68 different tasks across four LLM families. Similar error patterns appear in multi-step agentic scenarios, with consistent 5–10x misestimation of total duration. Despite this, models correctly state propositional facts about typical durations from their training data, indicating that the issue is not world-knowledge but self-related timing.

Conclusion: LLMs, even advanced ones, currently lack a grounded sense of their own inference time and cannot reliably estimate how long their tasks or action sequences will take. Their time predictions are biased, noisy, and easily misled by superficial cues like stated complexity labels. This gap between propositional knowledge about time and experiential awareness of their own computation imposes practical limitations on using LLMs as autonomous planners or schedulers in time-sensitive contexts. Designers of agent systems must not rely on models’ internal duration estimates and should instead use external timing, monitoring, and scheduling mechanisms until better-grounded self-timing capabilities are developed.

Post-hoc recall is disconnected from reality -- estimates diverge from actuals by an order of magnitude in either direction.
