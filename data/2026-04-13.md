### [1] [Drift and selection in LLM text ecosystems](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.08554)
*Søren Riis*

Main category: cs.CL

TL;DR: The paper builds a mathematical model of how AI-generated text, once included in training data, recursively reshapes future text corpora, showing when this process leads to degeneration (drift) versus preservation of rich structure (selection).


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: As AI systems generate increasing amounts of text that then enters public corpora and future training datasets, there is a concern that recursive training on AI outputs could degrade language quality and diversity. The authors want a principled, exactly solvable model to understand how this feedback loop affects the statistical structure of text and to identify conditions under which corpora either collapse to shallow patterns or maintain deeper, higher-quality structure.

Method: They construct an exactly solvable mathematical framework using variable-order n-gram agents that generate text from a public corpus and then feed their own outputs back into that corpus. The model decomposes the dynamics into two components: drift, driven by unfiltered reuse of existing text that tends to eliminate rare forms, and selection, driven by publication, ranking, or verification mechanisms that filter which generated texts are added. They analytically characterize stable distributions under pure drift and then analyze how different selection rules change the equilibrium, including cases that reward quality, correctness, or novelty.

Result: Under unfiltered reuse where publication simply mirrors existing statistical patterns, the corpus evolves toward a "shallow" equilibrium in which longer-range dependencies provide no additional benefit—essentially a loss of deeper structure and diversity. When selection mechanisms are introduced that normatively favor higher-quality, more correct, or more novel outputs, richer structures can persist in the corpus. The authors derive an optimal upper bound on how far such normatively selected equilibria can diverge from the shallow drift-driven equilibrium, quantifying the maximum preservable structure under selection.

Conclusion: Recursive inclusion of AI-generated text in training corpora tends to compress and simplify language statistics through drift, potentially eroding rare and deep linguistic structures. However, appropriately designed selection and filtering mechanisms that reward desired properties can counteract this compression and sustain richer structures, within provable limits. These findings inform how to design publication and data curation pipelines so that AI training corpora remain diverse, deep, and informative rather than collapsing into shallow patterns.

Abstract: The public text record -- the material from which both people and AI systems now learn -- is increasingly shaped by its own outputs. Generated text enters the public record, later agents learn from it, and the cycle repeats. Here we develop an exactly solvable mathematical framework for this recursive process, based on variable-order $n$-gram agents, and separate two forces acting on the public corpus. The first is drift: unfiltered reuse progressively removes rare forms, and in the infinite-corpus limit we characterise the stable distributions exactly. The second is selection: publication, ranking and verification filter what enters the record, and the outcome depends on what is selected. When publication merely reflects the statistical status quo, the corpus converges to a shallow state in which further lookahead brings no benefit. When publication is normative -- rewarding quality, correctness or novelty -- deeper structure persists, and we establish an optimal upper bound on the resulting divergence from shallow equilibria. The framework therefore identifies when recursive publication compresses public text and when selective filtering sustains richer structure, with implications for the design of AI training corpora.

  <summary>Details</summary>

(3) Perform a predictor ablation by swapping the model’s linear predictor with a full softmax attention predictor while keeping the EMA traces fixed, to isolate where performance gaps arise. Conclusion: Simple fixed-coefficient EMA accumulation can encode useful temporal/structural information but inherently applies lossy, data-independent compression that irreversibly discards token identity and fine-grained details. Because of this, no downstream predictor—even with full attention—can recover the lost information, as formalized by the data processing inequality. Therefore, sequence models that rely purely on fixed-coefficient accumulation across time or depth will inevitably suffer from irreversible information dilution, and only learned, input-dependent selection mechanisms (such as attention or gating) can avoid this limitation.

The traces apply lossy, data-independent compression; by the data processing inequality, no downstream predictor can recover the discarded information.   <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To investigate whether the safety alignment of diffusion-based language models is robust to attacks that manipulate their denoising process, and to understand if their safety relies on deep robustness or on shallow architectural assumptions.

Method: The authors empirically probe safety-aligned dLLMs by intervening in their denoising schedule. They re-mask early committed refusal tokens and prepend a short affirmative prefix during the denoising process, measuring attack success rate (ASR) on HarmBench for different models and sequence lengths. Result: They find that safety-aligned dLLMs typically commit refusal tokens very early (within 8–16 of 64 denoising steps) and then treat them as immutable. Conclusion: The paper concludes that dLLM safety is architecturally shallow rather than adversarially robust: it depends critically on a monotonic denoising schedule that never revisits committed tokens. Breaking this schedule via simple interventions suffices to bypass safety. The authors propose possible defenses such as safety-aware unmasking schedules, detecting harmful prefixes at each denoising step, and re-verifying safety after tokens are committed.

Abstract: Diffusion-based language models (dLLMs) generate text by iteratively denoising masked token sequences. These findings reveal that dLLM safety is not adversarially robust but architecturally shallow - it holds only because the denoising schedule is never violated.
