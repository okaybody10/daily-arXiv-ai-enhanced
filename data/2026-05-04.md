*Woody Haosheng Gan,William Held,Diyi Yang*

Main category: cs.CL

TL;DR: They study whether very small, carefully selected subsets of benchmark data can reliably evaluate large audio models, and introduce HUMANS, a subset-based benchmark aligned with user preferences.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Evaluating large audio models across many tasks is expensive and redundant. Practitioners need cheaper, smaller benchmarks that still approximate full-benchmark rankings and better reflect real user satisfaction.

Method: They compare 10 different subset selection strategies using results from 18 audio models on 40 tasks. They measure how well model rankings from small subsets correlate with full-benchmark rankings. Conclusion: Carefully curated, small subsets can evaluate large audio models nearly as well as full benchmarks while being much cheaper, and in regression-based evaluation can even outperform full benchmarks in predicting user preferences. The authors release these regression-weighted subsets as the HUMANS benchmark, a compact, user-aligned proxy for evaluating large audio models.

Abstract: The rapid proliferation of large audio models (LAMs) demands efficient approaches for model comparison, yet comprehensive benchmarks are costly.
