### [1] [The Coin Flip Judge? Reliability and Bias in LLM-as-a-Judge Evaluation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13685)
*Abel Yagubyan*

Main category: cs.CL

TL;DR: Study shows LLM-as-a-Judge evaluations are noisy and inconsistent, making single-trial judgments unreliable.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: LLM-as-a-Judge is widely used for ranking model outputs and training reward models, but its run-to-run reliability and stability have not been thoroughly quantified. The authors want to understand how often identical evaluations yield different verdicts and what factors contribute to this variability, because such noise can undermine leaderboards and high-stakes evaluation pipelines.

Method: The authors repeatedly run identical evaluation queries (50 pairwise trials and 50 pointwise trials per question) over 29 tasks in 10 categories, using two OpenAI judge models (GPT-4o-mini and GPT-4.1-mini). They measure flip rates in pairwise preferences, analyze pointwise score gaps, test for positional bias, compare cross-judge agreement, and run ablations on prompts, temperature, and decoding strategy (including deterministic decoding). They also build reliability curves to estimate how many repeated trials are needed so that a majority vote converges to a 50-trial reference verdict with high probability.

Result: Pairwise preferences flip 13.6% of the time on average, with 28% of questions showing more than 20% flip rates and one question reaching 56% flips. GPT-4o-mini shows a statistically significant bias toward the first position (72% A-majority, p = 0.024). Pointwise score differences between answers are small (0.19–0.36 on a 10-point scale) and not statistically significant overall, yet the models often pick a winner in pairwise comparisons—revealing a mismatch between scalar scores and pairwise decisions. Cross-judge agreement is about 76% with moderate κ = 0.51. Prompt template variations that are semantically equivalent still change majority outcomes in 25% of cases. Deterministic decoding lowers but does not remove inconsistency. Reliability curve analysis indicates around 11 repeated trials are needed for a 95% chance of matching the 50-trial reference verdict on average, and about 15 trials for high-variance questions.

Conclusion: LLM-based judges, as currently used, are noticeably noisy and sensitive to factors like random seed, positional ordering, prompt wording, and judge model choice. Single-run judgments are therefore unreliable for high-stakes or fine-grained evaluation. The authors recommend multi-trial aggregation (e.g., majority vote over many runs), randomizing the order of candidate outputs, and reporting uncertainty as standard practice. They also note that their study is limited to models from a single provider, so cross-provider replication is needed to fully characterize LLM-as-a-Judge reliability.

Abstract: LLM-as-a-Judge is now widely used to rank model outputs, train reward models, and populate public leaderboards, but its run-to-run reliability remains under-characterized. We study repeated identical evaluations on 29 tasks spanning 10 categories using two OpenAI judge models (GPT-4o-mini and GPT-4.1-mini), with 50 pairwise trials and 50 pointwise trials per question, supplemented by temperature and prompt-sensitivity ablations. Across judges, pairwise preferences flip on average 13.6% of the time, with 28% of questions exceeding a 20% flip rate and one question reaching 56%. GPT-4o-mini also exhibits a significant first-position bias (72% A-majority, p = 0.024). At the same time, mean pointwise score gaps are small (0.19--0.36 on a 10-point scale) and not statistically significant in aggregate, producing a pairwise--pointwise gap: judges frequently choose a winner even when their own scalar scores provide little evidence of a meaningful quality difference. Beyond within-judge instability, cross-judge agreement is only 76% ($κ= 0.51$), semantically equivalent prompt templates change majority outcomes in 25% of tested cases, and deterministic decoding reduces but does not eliminate inconsistency. A reliability curve analysis shows that, in our dataset, 11 repeated trials are needed for a majority vote to recover the 50-trial reference verdict with 95% probability on average, rising to 15 for high-variance questions. These findings suggest that single-trial LLM judging is often too noisy for high-stakes evaluation, and that multi-trial aggregation, position randomization, and explicit uncertainty reporting should be standard practice. Because both judges are from a single provider, cross-provider replication remains an important next step.
