*Marcelo A. Montemurro,Mirko Degli Esposti*

Main category: cs.CL

TL;DR: The paper proposes a surrogate-generation method for symbolic sequences that simultaneously preserves symbol frequencies and long-range correlations characterized by DFA scaling, enabling principled tests of structural features in language, DNA, and other symbolic systems.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Symbolic sequences like text and DNA exhibit both specific frequency distributions (e.g., Zipf's law, base composition) and long-range correlations or memory. Existing surrogate models typically preserve only one of these aspects at a time, limiting their usefulness for testing hypotheses about the origin of scaling laws and memory. A method that preserves both would allow more rigorous analysis and disentangling of structural features in symbolic data.

Method: The authors construct surrogate symbolic sequences by first generating fractional Gaussian noise (FGN) with a specified long-range dependence structure (DFA exponent), and then mapping the continuous FGN values onto discrete symbols via an assignment that exactly matches the empirical symbol frequency histogram of the original sequence. Result: The method successfully generates surrogate sequences that match the original data in both symbol frequency distribution and DFA scaling exponent, while differing in local structure. Validation on English and Latin texts shows that word frequencies and long-range correlations are preserved, and application to genomic DNA demonstrates accurate reproduction of base composition and long-memory scaling. The surrogates thus provide controlled synthetic counterparts to real symbolic sequences.

Conclusion: The proposed surrogate-generation framework offers a principled way to preserve both first-order statistics and long-range correlations in symbolic sequences, while destroying short-range structure. This makes it a powerful tool for probing which features of language, DNA, and other symbolic systems are responsible for observed scaling laws and memory effects, and for testing competing models of their generative mechanisms across diverse domains.

Abstract: Symbolic sequences such as written language and genomic DNA display characteristic frequency distributions and long-range correlations extending over many symbols. Existing surrogate models usually preserve either the frequency distribution or the correlation properties, but not both simultaneously.

Our method generates surrogates of symbolic sequences by mapping fractional Gaussian noise (FGN) onto the empirical histogram through a frequency-preserving assignment. The resulting surrogates match the original in first-order statistics and long-range scaling while randomising short-range dependencies.
