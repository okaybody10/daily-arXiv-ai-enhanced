### [1] [Elderly-Contextual Data Augmentation via Speech Synthesis for Elderly ASR](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24770)
*Minsik Lee,Seoi Hong,Chongmin Lee,Sieun Choi,Jian Kim,Jua Han,Jihie Kim*

Main category: cs.CL

TL;DR: They improve elderly speech recognition by augmenting scarce training data using LLM-based paraphrasing plus TTS to synthesize elderly-style speech, then fine-tune Whisper, achieving large WER reductions.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Elderly automatic speech recognition (EASR) is harder than standard ASR because elderly speech has distinct acoustic and linguistic traits, while available elderly datasets are small. Existing models like Whisper perform poorly without enough in-domain training data. The authors want a practical way to boost EASR without redesigning ASR architectures or requiring large amounts of real elderly recordings.

Method: Starting from a real elderly speech dataset, they (1) use a large language model to generate paraphrases of the original transcripts that keep elderly-related context but vary wording; (2) use a TTS system conditioned on elderly reference speakers to synthesize speech for those paraphrased texts, creating new elderly-style audio; (3) merge the synthetic elderly audio-text pairs with the original data to fine-tune Whisper, keeping Whisper’s architecture unchanged. They systematically vary the augmentation ratio (amount of synthetic vs. real data) and the composition of elderly reference speakers to study their impacts in low-resource EASR scenarios.

Result: On English and Korean datasets with speakers aged 70+, their augmentation pipeline consistently outperforms standard augmentation baselines. When fine-tuning Whisper with the augmented data, they obtain up to a 58.2% reduction in word error rate compared with the un-augmented Whisper baseline, showing strong gains across both languages and settings studied.

Conclusion: LLM-driven elderly-context paraphrasing combined with elderly-style TTS synthesis is an effective data augmentation strategy for low-resource elderly ASR. It significantly enhances Whisper’s performance without architectural changes and outperforms conventional augmentation methods. The approach is language-agnostic and suggests that carefully designed synthetic data can address demographic-specific ASR challenges when real data is scarce.

Abstract: Despite recent progress in automatic speech recognition (ASR), elderly ASR (EASR) remains challenging due to limited training data and the distinct acoustic and linguistic characteristics of elderly speech. In this work, we address data scarcity in EASR through a data augmentation pipeline that combines large language model (LLM)-based transcript paraphrasing with text-to-speech (TTS) synthesis. Given an elderly speech dataset, the LLM first generates elderly-contextual paraphrases of the original transcripts, and the TTS model then synthesizes corresponding speech using elderly reference speakers. We further analyze the effects of augmentation ratio and reference-speaker composition in low-resource EASR. Experiments on English and Korean elderly speech datasets from speakers aged 70 and above show that the proposed method consistently improves performance over conventional augmentation baselines, achieving up to a 58.2% reduction in word error rate (WER) compared with the Whisper baseline.

### [2] [Large Language Models Explore by Latent Distilling](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24927)
*Yuanhao Zeng,Ao Lu,Lufei Li,Zheng Zhang,Yexin Li,Kan Ren*

Main category: cs.CL

TL;DR: The paper introduces Exploratory Sampling (ESamp), a decoding method for large language models that increases semantic (not just lexical) diversity by using prediction error from a shallow-to-deep representation distiller as a novelty signal during generation, improving Pass@k and diversity–coherence trade-offs with minimal overhead.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Standard stochastic decoding (e.g., temperature, top-k/p) mostly creates superficial lexical variation and fails to thoroughly explore different semantic reasoning paths or ideas, which is especially limiting for test-time scaling strategies that rely on multiple diverse samples (e.g., for reasoning, math, and code). The authors are motivated by the need for a principled way to encourage exploration of semantically novel continuations while maintaining coherence and with low computational overhead at inference time, leveraging the known property that neural networks exhibit higher prediction error on out-of-distribution or novel inputs.

Method: They propose Exploratory Sampling (ESamp). First, they train at test time a lightweight Distiller network that predicts deep-layer hidden states of the LLM from its shallow-layer representations, effectively modeling depth-wise representation transitions. This Distiller is continuously updated during generation using an asynchronous training–inference pipeline. For each candidate next token, ESamp uses the Distiller to predict the deep representation and compares it with the actual deep hidden state, using the prediction error as a novelty measure: larger error indicates a more semantically novel continuation. ESamp then reweights the token probabilities according to this novelty signal, biasing decoding toward candidates with higher predicted novelty while still respecting the base LLM probabilities. The whole process adds <5% worst-case and ~1–2% typical runtime overhead.

Result: Across mathematics, science, and code reasoning benchmarks, ESamp improves Pass@k efficiency over strong baselines including standard stochastic sampling and heuristic diversity-enhancing methods, meaning it attains higher success rates for a given number of samples. It also shows robust generalization across domains, not just on the tasks used for tuning. In creative writing evaluations, ESamp generates outputs that are both more diverse and comparably or more coherent than baselines, effectively relaxing the usual diversity–coherence trade-off. Implementation results indicate that the asynchronous distiller training keeps computational overhead very low (<5%, ~1.2% optimized).

Conclusion: ESamp demonstrates that using internal representation prediction error as a novelty signal is an effective way to guide test-time sampling toward semantically diverse yet coherent generations. By adding a small auxiliary Distiller trained on-the-fly to approximate deep-layer states from shallow ones, the method systematically explores less-visited semantic regions of the model’s hypothesis space, leading to better multi-sample reasoning performance and improved diversity without sacrificing coherence. The approach is efficient, generalizes across several domains, and is practical to integrate into existing LLM decoding pipelines.

Abstract: Generating diverse responses is crucial for test-time scaling of large language models (LLMs), yet standard stochastic sampling mostly yields surface-level lexical variation, limiting semantic exploration. In this paper, we propose Exploratory Sampling (ESamp), a decoding approach that explicitly encourages semantic diversity during generation. ESamp is motivated by the well-known observation that neural networks tend to make lower-error predictions on inputs similar to those encountered before, and incur higher prediction error on novel ones. Building on this property, we train a lightweight Distiller at test time to predict deep-layer hidden representations of the LLM from its shallow-layer representations to model the LLM's depth-wise representation transitions. During decoding, the Distiller continuously adapts to the mappings induced by the current generation context. ESamp uses the prediction error as a novelty signal to reweight candidate token extensions conditioned on the current prefix, thereby biasing decoding toward less-explored semantic patterns. ESamp is implemented with an asynchronous training--inference pipeline, with less than 5% worst case overhead (1.2% in the optimized release). Empirical results show that ESamp significantly boosts the Pass@k efficiency of reasoning models, showing superior or comparable performance to strong stochastic and heuristic baselines. Notably, ESamp achieves robust generalization across mathematics, science, and code generation benchmarks and breaks the trade-off between diversity and coherence in creative writing. Our code has released at: https://github.com/LinesHogan/tLLM.
