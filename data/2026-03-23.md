*Ruxiao Chen,Xilei Zhao,Thomas J. Cova,Frank A. Drews,Susu Xu*

Main category: cs.AI

TL;DR: They propose a structured cognitive trajectory model that gives LLMs a dynamic Theory of Mind by representing people’s beliefs as evolving probabilistic graphs, improving prediction and interpretability in disaster-evacuation tasks.

Motivation: Existing LLM-based Theory of Mind methods either rely purely on prompting or on simplistic latent-state models that assume static, independent beliefs. These lead to incoherent mental models over time, and poor reasoning in dynamic, high-uncertainty, high-stakes settings like disaster response. There is a need for a principled way to capture how human beliefs evolve, interact, and drive information seeking and decisions over time, in a form that can augment LLMs.

Method: They build a structured cognitive trajectory model where a person’s mental state is encoded as a dynamic belief graph. The model: (1) maps textual probabilistic statements into consistent updates of a probabilistic graphical model; (2) represents interdependencies among beliefs with an energy-based factor graph that can capture time-varying structure; and (3) uses an ELBO-based training objective that jointly infers latent beliefs, their temporal evolution, and the link between belief states, information-seeking steps, and actions, including delayed decisions. This module is designed to plug into LLM-based ToM pipelines.

Result: On several real-world disaster-evacuation datasets, the model yields significantly better action prediction accuracy compared to prior ToM and baseline approaches. It also recovers interpretable trajectories of beliefs over time that align with human-like reasoning about evolving risk, information, and choices under uncertainty.

Conclusion: Modeling Theory of Mind for LLMs as an evolving probabilistic belief graph, rather than static or independent latent states, yields more coherent and predictive mental models in dynamic, uncertain environments. Their structured cognitive trajectory module provides a principled, interpretable way to augment LLMs with time-evolving ToM, particularly useful for high-stakes domains like disaster response where understanding and predicting human decisions is critical.
